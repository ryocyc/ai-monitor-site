OpenAI Makes ChatGPT Free for Verified U.S. Clinicians

OpenAI has released a dedicated, no-cost version of ChatGPT for verified physicians, nurse practitioners, physician assistants, and pharmacists in the United States.

OpenAI Newsroom 2026-04-23 07:07 UTC Key: openai-newsroom::identity::making-chatgpt-better-for-clinicians Confidence: strong Mode: claude

Article body

The product, called ChatGPT for Clinicians, is built for tasks like medical documentation, care consultation, and literature review. It grants free access to OpenAI's frontier models, cited search across peer-reviewed medical sources, and a feature that converts repeatable workflows—like referral letters or prior authorization requests—into reusable skills. Clinicians can also earn continuing medical education credits by having ChatGPT track their evidence reviews. Optional HIPAA compliance is available through a Business Associate Agreement for eligible accounts.

The launch reflects a sharp rise in AI adoption among U.S. clinicians. An American Medical Association survey cited by OpenAI found that 72% of physicians now use AI in clinical practice, up from 48% the prior year. The company says millions of clinicians worldwide already use ChatGPT weekly, with usage more than doubling over the past year. OpenAI's physician advisors reviewed over 700,000 model responses during development, and a pre-release test involving 6,924 conversations saw physicians rate 99.6% of responses as safe and accurate. GPT‑5.4, the model powering the offering, also outperformed human physicians in a benchmark comparison where doctors had unbounded time and web access.

ChatGPT for Clinicians is available immediately at no charge to verified U.S. clinicians. OpenAI says it plans to expand access to additional countries over time, beginning with a pilot through the Better Evidence Network. A HealthBench Professional benchmark and a Health Blueprint document were published alongside the release.

Why this matters

  • Free access removes a cost barrier for individual clinicians who previously had to rely on general-purpose ChatGPT or enterprise licensing.
  • The release signals deepening competition among AI providers for the healthcare market, alongside offerings from Google Health, Microsoft, and others.
  • The claimed performance advantage over human physicians in a timed benchmark is a notable claim that will draw scrutiny from clinicians and researchers.

Source note

  • The article is drawn directly from OpenAI's announcement page, published April 22, 2026, on the company's newsroom. OpenAI has a commercial interest in presenting its product favorably, and the cited physician rating of 99.6% safety and the benchmark comparison to human doctors come from the company's own testing rather than an independent evaluation. Independent third-party benchmarks like Stanford's MedHELM are referenced but are not described in detail on this page.
