Grok Speech to Text and Text to Speech APIs

xAI has released two standalone audio APIs — Grok Speech to Text and Grok Text to Speech — built on the same stack that powers Grok Voice in Tesla vehicles and Starlink customer support.

xAI Blog 2026-04-22 23:49 UTC Key: xai-blog::identity::grok-stt-and-tts-apis Confidence: strong Mode: claude

Article body

The Grok Speech to Text API handles both batch transcription of large audio files and real-time streaming, offering word-level timestamps, speaker diarization, and multichannel support. It also performs Inverse Text Normalization, converting raw spoken language into structured output for numbers, dates, currencies, and other entities. The API currently covers 25 or more languages, allowing seamless switching mid-session.

Benchmark data published alongside the release shows Grok STT achieving an overall word error rate of 6.9%, compared to 9.0% for ElevenLabs, 11.0% for Deepgram, and 12.9% for AssemblyAI. On phone call entity recognition — a task xAI frames as relevant to medical, legal, and financial use cases — Grok STT reached 5.0% error versus a range of 12.0–21.3% for the competing models.

On the synthesis side, Grok Text to Speech supports fine-grained delivery control via inline speech tags such as [laugh], [sigh], [whisper], and more, allowing developers to shape prosody and emotion without complex markup. Both APIs are accessible via REST and WebSocket interfaces. Pricing is straightforward: $0.10 per hour for batch STT, $0.20 per hour for streaming STT, and $4.20 per million characters for TTS.

Why this matters

  • xAI is positioning Grok STT and TTS as direct competitors to established speech infrastructure providers, backed by benchmark figures that favor its accuracy across phone calls, meetings, video, and telephony.
  • The APIs share a technology base with Grok Voice as deployed in Tesla vehicles and Starlink support, suggesting real-world robustness beyond standard evaluation datasets.
  • Simple, flat-rate pricing without volume tiers or hidden fees could lower the barrier for developers building voice-enabled applications at any scale.
