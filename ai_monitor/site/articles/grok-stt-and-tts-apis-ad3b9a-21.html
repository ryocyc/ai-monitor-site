xAI Launches Grok Speech to Text and Text to Speech APIs

xAI has released two standalone audio APIs—Grok Speech to Text and Grok Text to Speech—bringing its speech technology to developers outside of Grok Voice.

xAI Blog 2026-04-22 23:49 UTC Key: xai-blog::identity::grok-stt-and-tts-apis Confidence: strong Mode: claude

Article body

xAI has released two standalone audio APIs—Grok Speech to Text (STT) and Grok Text to Speech (TTS). The APIs, announced April 17, 2026, are built on the same infrastructure powering Grok Voice in Tesla vehicles and Starlink customer support. Developers can now integrate these endpoints directly without relying on the Grok chatbot itself, opening use cases in voice agents, real-time transcription, accessibility tools, podcasts, and interactive audio.

Grok STT handles batch transcription via REST API and real-time streaming via WebSocket, offering features like word-level timestamps, speaker diarization, multichannel support, and intelligent Inverse Text Normalization for numbers, dates, and currencies. A benchmark table on the announcement page claims an overall Word Error Rate of 6.9%, compared to 9.0% for ElevenLabs, 11.0% for Deepgram, and 12.9% for AssemblyAI. The STT API also lists support for 25+ languages with seamless switching.

Grok TTS generates speech via REST and WebSocket APIs and adds inline speech tags—`[laugh]`, `[sigh]`, `[whisper]`, and others—to control prosody and emotion. Pricing is straightforward: STT at $0.10 per audio hour for batch and $0.20 per hour for streaming; TTS at $4.20 per one million characters. Both products point to the xAI API console and documentation for further details.

Why this matters

  • xAI is now competing directly in the speech infrastructure market, offering APIs outside the Grok chatbot interface for the first time.
  • Grok STT claims a lower Word Error Rate than ElevenLabs, Deepgram, and AssemblyAI across phone calls, meetings, video/podcasts, and telephony, according to the company's own benchmarks.
  • Simple flat-rate pricing and speech tags for prosody control make this a target for developers building voice agents, accessibility tools, and interactive audio products.
