OpenAI Makes ChatGPT Free for Verified U.S. Clinicians

OpenAI is offering a free, clinically focused version of ChatGPT to verified physicians, nurse practitioners, physician assistants, and pharmacists in the United States.

OpenAI Newsroom 2026-04-23 07:07 UTC Key: openai-newsroom::identity::making-chatgpt-better-for-clinicians Confidence: strong Mode: claude

Article body

OpenAI has launched ChatGPT for Clinicians, a version of its AI assistant tailored for medical tasks such as documentation, medical research, and care consultations, and made it available at no cost to verified U.S. clinicians. The offer covers physicians, nurse practitioners (NPs), physician assistants (PAs), and pharmacists, with plans to expand internationally in collaboration with the Better Evidence Network as local regulations permit. Clinicians can access the service at chatgpt.com/cfc-plan.

The announcement comes alongside data OpenAI says shows rapid clinician adoption: a 2026 American Medical Association survey found 72% of physicians now use AI in clinical practice, up from 48% the prior year, and the company reports that clinician usage of ChatGPT has more than doubled over the past twelve months. ChatGPT for Clinicians includes access to frontier AI models, reusable "skills" for repeatable tasks like prior authorization letters and patient instructions, cited answers drawn from peer-reviewed medical sources, deep research across medical journals, optional HIPAA compliance through a Business Associate Agreement (BAA), and the ability for eligible evidence review to count toward continuing medical education (CME) credits. Conversations are not used to train models, and the company says protections such as multi-factor authentication are in place.

OpenAI also released HealthBench Professional, an open benchmark for evaluating AI on real clinician chat tasks across care consultation, documentation, and medical research. Prior to launch, physician advisors reviewed more than 700,000 model responses reflecting real-world clinical use, and rated 99.6% of responses as safe and accurate across 6,924 pre-release conversations. In a subset of 355 examples where three independent physicians specified ground-truth citations, ChatGPT for Clinicians cited those sources more often than human physicians. The company says GPT‑5.4, the model powering the service, outperforms base GPT‑5.4, all other OpenAI and external models, and human physicians on the benchmark. OpenAI simultaneously published a Health Blueprint outlining recommendations for responsible AI integration in U.S. healthcare.

Why this matters

  • The majority of U.S. physicians now report using AI in clinical practice, making the availability of a free, clinically targeted AI tool a potentially significant shift in how clinicians access decision support and administrative relief.
  • OpenAI's claims of benchmark performance exceeding human physicians on citation accuracy and response quality signal a new level of AI capability in medical contexts, though direct comparison methodology warrants scrutiny.
  • By offering free access alongside optional HIPAA compliance, OpenAI is attempting to lower the barrier for individual clinicians while also positioning ChatGPT for Clinicians for adoption by health systems that require regulatory safeguards.
