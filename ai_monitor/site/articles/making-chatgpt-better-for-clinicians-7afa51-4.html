OpenAI Launches Free ChatGPT for Verified U.S. Clinicians

OpenAI is making ChatGPT available for free to verified physicians, nurse practitioners, physician assistants, and pharmacists in the United States, targeting the growing administrative and research burden on healthcare workers.

OpenAI Newsroom 2026-04-23 07:07 UTC Key: openai-newsroom::identity::making-chatgpt-better-for-clinicians Confidence: strong Mode: claude

Article body

OpenAI on Tuesday launched ChatGPT for Clinicians, a version of its AI assistant built specifically for healthcare tasks, and is offering it at no cost to verified individual clinicians in the U.S. The product targets documentation, medical research, and care consultations—workflows where clinicians say they are spending increasing amounts of time. Eligibility is limited to physicians, NPs, PAs, and pharmacists; expansion to other countries is planned, beginning with a pilot via the Better Evidence Network.

The announcement arrives alongside a 2026 American Medical Association survey cited by OpenAI showing physician AI adoption has climbed to 72%, up from 48% the prior year. According to OpenAI, clinician usage of ChatGPT has more than doubled over the past year. The new product bundles several features not previously available together in one offering: cited answers drawn from peer-reviewed medical sources, deep research across medical journals with user-selected trusted sources, reusable skills for repeatable tasks like prior authorization letters and patient instructions, and automatic tracking of evidence review toward continuing medical education credits. HIPAA support via a Business Associate Agreement is available as an optional add-on.

OpenAI is also releasing HealthBench Professional, an open benchmark for clinician chat tasks covering care consult, documentation, and research. Before launch, physician advisors tested 6,924 conversations in real clinical settings and rated 99.6% of responses as safe and accurate. On a curated subset of 355 difficult cases reviewed by three independent physicians, GPT‑5.4 cited the correct sources more often than the physicians themselves. OpenAI says GPT‑5.4 outperformed both external models and human physicians across the benchmark, though the system is positioned as a support tool, not a replacement for clinical judgment.

Why this matters

  • The majority of U.S. physicians now use AI in clinical practice, making fit-for-purpose tools increasingly critical.
  • Free individual access lowers the barrier for solo practitioners and independent clinicians who lack enterprise contracts.
  • The concurrent release of an open benchmark (HealthBench Professional) offers the medical and AI research community a shared yardstick for tracking progress in clinical AI.
